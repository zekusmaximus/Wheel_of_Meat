# Scene 5: The Next Thread

---

Dawn came to Manchester the way it always came in October: slowly, reluctantly, the darkness thinning to grey before admitting any actual light. Lilith stood at her bedroom window and watched the city emerge from night, the brick buildings solidifying from shadow, the streets revealing their emptiness before filling with the early movements of people who had places to be.

She had not slept.

After leaving the tracking corner she had thought she would—had felt the exhaustion in her body, the particular heaviness that came after extended work across time. But when she lay down, her mind would not settle. The revelations of the night kept surfacing: the weight of ten thousand bodies, the threshold perceived, the commitment made.

So she had risen after twenty minutes of restless lying and returned to the window to watch Manchester wake.

Her body ached. The headache behind her left eye had faded to a dull presence. Her shoulders carried their familiar tension. Six years of tracking had embedded themselves in muscle memory, and one night of revelatory clarity—however profound—would not undo the physical patterns that sustained work had carved into flesh.

But her mind was clear. Clearer than it had been in months. Perhaps years.

She knew what she was now. Knew where she stood. Knew what she had to do.

The certainty was almost peaceful. Would be peaceful if it were not shadowed by the knowledge that certainty did not guarantee success, that understanding the pattern did not automatically confer the power to change it.

But it was a beginning. The first necessary step: seeing clearly.

The city's color returned as dawn strengthened. The brick shifted from grey to its daytime red-brown. The pavement dried where October cold had left frost. A bus passed below her window, its engine sound the first mechanical noise of the day, and she watched it carry people toward destinations she would never know, lives she would never track, souls cycling through their own patterns in ignorance of the wheel beneath them.

She had been one of them once. Before Chapter 3, before the first discovery, before she knew what Silas was or what she was or that they were both approaching the same threshold from different angles.

That ignorance felt distant now. Impossible to return to. Like trying to unsee a pattern once perceived—the mind could not go back to not knowing what it knew.

As dawn light strengthened, she felt it: the flicker of his thread returning to accessibility.

---

The sensation was familiar after six years of tracking. Between lives there was a gap—a period where consciousness burned through the transition from one incarnation to the next and Lilith's awareness could not reach it. The gap varied in duration: sometimes hours, sometimes days. With Diego it had been—

She checked the notebook she had carried from the tracking corner, found the entry, counted.

Sixteen hours since his death at the stake. The thread had gone dark immediately after his final breath, consciousness burning forward into whatever space existed between meat and meat.

And now: returning. The thread becoming accessible again. Not a life yet—nothing formed enough to observe with clarity. But the shape of it beginning to coalesce, the pattern gathering itself toward the next configuration.

Lilith sat on the edge of her bed and reached.

The tracking was easier at these early stages, before the new life solidified into full incarnation with its sensory weight and circumstantial complexity. She could perceive pattern without being overwhelmed by detail. Could see the shape of what was forming without having to inhabit it.

The thread pulled toward: France. Renaissance France. The image formed not as place but as texture—the particular quality of a culture in transformation, old certainties crumbling, new frameworks not yet established. The fertile chaos of transition.

Not a city. Smaller. A town? A university? The specifics were not yet clear. But the flavor was unmistakable: intellectual ferment, questioning, the atmosphere of a place where ideas mattered and could be dangerous.

And at the center of the forming thread: a mind that would ask questions.

The resonance with Diego's final thought was immediate. *What else can be questioned?* The hunger that had burned brighter than flames, that had pulled consciousness forward with the force of gravity toward a life where doubt would be permitted, where skepticism would be virtue rather than heresy.

The seed had taken root.

Lilith traced the pattern forming: a philosopher, though not in the ancient Greek sense. No academy, no formal school. Something newer. A man—the gender felt clear even at this early stage—who would doubt not for doubt's sake but to find what remained when all comfortable certainties had been stripped away.

A skeptic. The word arrived in her awareness with the click of recognition. Not just someone who questioned, but someone who made questioning the foundation of method. Who would examine every belief, test every assumption, trust nothing that could not survive rigorous doubt.

Diego's release of hatred had seeded this. His recognition that everything could be questioned had burned forward into a life where that recognition would become technique, would become philosophy, would become the work itself.

The progression was elegant. Inevitable. The kind of karmic bridge that made the wheel's structure visible—each life planting seeds for the next, each death carrying forward the lesson learned, each new incarnation building on foundations the previous life had laid.

If she tried to corrupt his doubt—to make him doubt the wrong things, to push him toward nihilism or despair or the kind of radical skepticism that dissolved into paralysis—it would backfire. She could already see the pattern. Interference would refine him. Would sharpen his method. Would teach him to distinguish between productive doubt and corrosive doubt, between questioning that clarified and questioning that simply destroyed.

The backfire was predictable now. Structural. She could map it before attempting intervention.

Which meant—

What? That she should not intervene at all?

The thought surfaced and sank. Not intervening meant letting him progress unimpeded. Meant watching him move toward the threshold while she stood still or moved more slowly. Meant accepting that he might finish first and she would cycle again.

Unacceptable.

But intervening through the old methods meant accelerating him. Six years of evidence. Six failures. The pattern was undeniable.

New approach required.

The thought circled back to what she had written in her notebook: *Stop working through intermediaries. Consider direct contact.*

For six years she had tracked his past lives. Had watched him cycle through incarnations, had intervened in historical moments four centuries or more removed from her present. Had worked through vessels and circumstances and the limitations of reaching backward through time.

But he existed now. In this time. In a body she had never tracked because she had been so focused on his past that she had not thought to look for his present.

Somewhere in the world—in Manchester, perhaps, or elsewhere in Britain, or perhaps not even born yet depending on the timing—there was a man who carried Diego's consciousness forward, who would someday be Silas Pahlavan, who was already approaching the threshold from his current position on the wheel.

She could find him.

The tracking ability that allowed her to perceive threads across time could be turned toward present-day search. She had never attempted it because the work had always been about his past, about understanding his progression, about finding points of intervention in incarnations already completed.

But if the past was closed to effective intervention—if indirect methods were structurally prevented from working—then perhaps the present was open.

Direct contact. Direct relationship. Not working through vessels but through herself. Not observing from four centuries' distance but from across a room, a table, the small space between two bodies both wearing temporary flesh in the same present moment.

The possibility opened in her awareness like dawn light strengthening.

She would still track the past. Would still need to understand his progression, the lessons he had learned, the patterns that shaped him. The Renaissance skeptic would teach him something she would need to know, would refine some capacity that would carry forward into his current incarnation.

But her intervention—if she intervened, when she intervened—might need to happen not in his past but in hers. Their shared present. The only moment where direct contact was possible.

The strategic shift was fundamental. Changed everything about how she had been working. Required capabilities she had not yet developed—how did one find a specific soul in present-day incarnation? How did one engineer a meeting that seemed natural rather than suspicious? How did one establish the kind of proximity that would allow precision while maintaining the appearance of coincidence?

She did not know. But she had time to learn. The Renaissance skeptic would not complete his incarnation for decades. She had years—perhaps a decade or more—before his current-day incarnation aged into the configuration she needed to find. Time enough to develop new methods. To practice present-day tracking. To prepare for an approach that terrified and intrigued her in equal measure.

Direct contact meant visibility. Meant risk. Meant putting herself within his awareness in ways that could backfire just as spectacularly as past interventions had.

But it also meant precision. Meant seeing him clearly instead of guessing from distance. Meant the possibility—however slim—of intervention that worked because it was based on actual understanding rather than blind strategic strikes.

Worth considering. Worth preparing for. Worth the risk.

Maybe.

---

The dawn had fully arrived. Manchester was awake now—buses running, people moving, the city engaged in its daily work of being a city. Six-thirty in the morning. Lilith had been awake for more than twenty-four hours. The exhaustion was beginning to catch up with her body even as her mind remained crystalline with clarity.

She should sleep. Should let the revelations of this night settle into the kind of knowledge that lived in the body rather than just the mind. Should give herself time to integrate before making decisions that would shape years of work ahead.

But first—

She looked at her hands.

They were the same hands she had looked at yesterday. Same configuration of bone and flesh, same lines across palms, same slightly crooked left pinkie from a childhood break that had healed imperfectly. Thirty-one years of wear but still young by any reasonable measure. Still strong. Still capable.

How many hands had she had? How many configurations of fingers and palms and the particular way joints bent when making fists or gestures or the small movements that carried meaning?

Ten thousand pairs at least. Probably more. The somatic memory from last night's tracking was still vivid enough that she could almost feel them—the weight and variety, the particular textures each pair had known, the work each had done.

This pair of hands might be the last.

The thought arrived without drama. Simply recognition. If she finished—when she finished—this configuration would step off the wheel and these hands would be the ones that carried her to completion. No more hands after these. No more flesh. No more meat worn and discarded in endless cycling.

She spread her fingers against the window glass. The surface was cold from October night. Her palm left warmth that fogged the glass temporarily before fading.

These hands had written seventy-three notebooks. Had treated patients. Had reached across time to track souls she might never meet in person. Had carried her through six years of work that had changed her in ways she was only beginning to recognize.

They would carry her the rest of the way. To the threshold. To the finishing. To whatever came after the wheel stopped turning.

She believed that now. With the certainty of someone who had felt the pattern, who had perceived the structure, who knew what she was and where she stood.

The wheel turned beneath everything. But she could feel the edge.

And she was walking toward it.

One step at a time.

New methods. New strategies. New possibilities opening in the dawn light.

The work continued. But she was ready now.

Ready in ways she had not been ready before this night. Before she felt the wheel. Before she perceived the threshold. Before she committed to finishing without cycling again.

Everything had changed.

She pulled her hand back from the window. The warmth faded quickly, the glass returning to its uniform cold. The city continued its waking regardless of her revelations, regardless of wheels and thresholds and souls approaching completion.

That was appropriate. Most people would cycle through dozens more lives before approaching the awareness she held. Most would never approach it at all. The wheel would turn for them in ignorance, and that was not tragedy—it was simply pattern, the way things worked, the structure that governed all incarnation.

But she was not most people. She was close. And she would not let proximity slip away through inaction or failed strategy or the kind of indirect intervention that accelerated the very soul she was trying to slow.

New approach. Direct contact. Present-day work.

The thought both terrified and steadied her.

But it could wait. First: sleep. Then: integration. Then: planning.

The Renaissance skeptic would take decades to complete his incarnation. She had time.

The wheel turned slowly. But she was learning to turn with it deliberately, consciously, toward the edge that admitted those who were ready.

And she was ready.

Or she would be, by the time the next real intervention point arrived.

Lilith left the window and lay down on her bed. The exhaustion hit as soon as she was horizontal—twenty-four hours of waking, six hours of the deepest tracking she had ever done, revelations that had fundamentally changed how she understood herself and her work.

Sleep came quickly.

And with it: dreams of hands worn and discarded, bodies cycled through, threads running parallel toward a threshold that glowed in the distance like dawn breaking over a city she had never seen but somehow knew, the way one knows the shape of home even when home is still years away.

The wheel turned.

But the edge was visible now.

And she was walking toward it with eyes open, awareness clear, commitment certain.

One step at a time.

This life.

The last life.

Ready.
