## Temporal Structure

### Historical Thread: Wei Shufen (1862-1864)

Lilith tracks Wei across the two-year Siege of Tianjing. Unlike previous chapters where she observes key moments, here she **sustains intervention** across years—her most extensive manipulation.

**What she does:**
- Adjusts artillery timing so evacuees die
- Influences commanders to send healed soldiers to suicide missions
- Spreads disease to specific patients
- Causes intelligence failures and supply losses
- Creates the "curse" pattern: everyone Wei saves dies

**What she expects:** Wei will become demoralized, stop helping, determination broken.

**What actually happens:** The backfire. Wei's determination is *purified*, not broken. By making futility visible, she forces him to release outcome-attachment. True adhiṭṭhāna achieved.

### Contemporary Thread: Manchester, Autumn

She observes Wei from her Fallowfield flat during her tracking fever (Week 8-9). After Wei's death, she traces forward, senses Silas, and begins positioning—the chapter extends into her approaching his world.

**Manchester sensory palette:**
- Autumn rain on windows
- Institutional silence (student mental health offices)
- The violence of calm—waiting rooms, application forms, quiet desperation
- Leaves falling, the slow dying of the year
- Her flat: functional, sparse, a tracking station more than a home

## The "Many Lives" Framework

**Critical decision:** She has seen MANY past lives, not a numbered sequence.

- The ten historical avatars in the novel are the *significant* ones—the lessons-learned
- But she's glimpsed lives between them: brief deaths, moments, fragments
- She's seen Silas in forms we haven't—different faces, different eras
- When she looks at him in Ch 1, she sees a palimpsest of countless others

**In this chapter:** The vastness is implied, not shown. References to "many times," "again and again," "the pattern repeated across centuries." No specific count.

**Effect on prose:** Her exhaustion is deeper than nine attempts. Her statement in Ch 1 ("I've watched you die, over and over... cold and alone, numerous times") has earned weight.

This chapter is where the structural constraint becomes *felt*, not just believed.

During Wei's death or immediately after, she perceives something about the threshold:

- Not doctrine read in texts but pattern felt in structure

- The sense of running out of room

### The Tragic Irony

The pressure she feels is actually the *pull toward merger*—two halves of Maitreya sensing each other. But she interprets it as threat, not recognition. Competition, not completion.
