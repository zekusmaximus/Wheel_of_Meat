The Attendant led me through corridors that smelled of dust and plum, always plum, until the fragrance thickened into something almost physical. By the time she slid open the panel to the eastern gallery, my throat burned with it.

"Please wait here." She bowed, the movement fluid despite the rawness of her hands. "The Lady will speak with you presently."

She withdrew. The panel closed. I stood alone in a room that should not have existed.

The eastern gallery opened onto a small interior garden, visible through latticed screens that filtered what little moonlight penetrated the overcast sky. Lanterns burned at precise intervals along the veranda—not the sputtering oil lamps of most estates, but something steadier, their flames unnaturally still despite the draft that crept beneath the screens. The tatami beneath my knees was fresh, the woven edge crisp as newly cut grass. Every surface gleamed.

I had seen grand houses before. This was different. Grand houses showed their age in honorable ways—a patina of use, the comfortable wear of generations. This room felt preserved, as though someone had stopped time at the moment of its completion and held it there by force of will.

Behind the *sudare* at the far end of the gallery, a shape moved. Fabric rustling against fabric. The creak of a low table being adjusted.

"You came." The voice that emerged was barely a voice at all—thin, reedy, punctuated by the labored breath of someone for whom speech required effort. "I did not... I was not certain you would."

I bowed toward the blind, though she could not see me any better than I could see her. "Your poem moved me, my lady. I could not stay away."

A pause. The sound of breathing, rough at the edges.

"The poem," she said. Her voice wavered on the last syllable, as if she were trying to remember something that kept slipping away. "Yes. I... wrote it. I think I wrote it."

The uncertainty should have troubled me. Instead, I found it charming—the humility of a true artist, unable to take credit for what the gods had channeled through her brush. I had met poets who remembered every verse they'd ever composed and recited them at every gathering. This was different. This was genuine.

"Will you honor me with another?" I kept my voice soft, the register I used with nervous horses and grieving widows. "Your words are unlike anything I have encountered at court."

Silence stretched between us. Behind the *sudare*, the shape shifted, and for a moment I thought she would refuse.

Then the voice changed.

"*The watcher at the gate—*"

It was not the same voice. The thin uncertainty had vanished, replaced by something deeper, richer, a timbre that seemed to bypass my ears entirely and settle in the base of my spine. The words came with the inevitability of water finding its level.

"*—does not know the hour.*  
*He counts the falling leaves,*  
*but leaves do not count him.*  
*When the moon outlasts the watcher—*  
*what then?*"

The final syllable hung in the incense-thickened air. I realized I had stopped breathing. My hands, folded in my lap, had clenched without my permission.

The poetry of the court celebrated beauty. It mourned impermanence. It played elegant games with seasonal imagery and layered allusion. What I had just heard did none of those things. It asked a question that I could not answer—and worse, a question I was not certain I wanted answered.

I could memorize this verse—recite it at the next gathering, claim it as anonymous discovery, watch the other poets struggle to match what they could never match. The thought formed and dissolved in the same breath. The poem had been given to me, not to the court. Sharing it would have been a kind of theft I could not commit.

"My lady," I managed. My voice came out rougher than I intended. "That was—"

I stopped. What I had meant to say was *extraordinary*. What I had felt was something more complicated: the verse was flawless in ways that violated every rule I had been taught about what flawlessness meant. It was beautiful and it was wrong—two truths that should have cancelled each other but did not. I found I could hold them both without needing to choose between them.

"Did I say something beautiful?"

The voice had changed again. Thin. Uncertain. Confused.

"I so rarely..." She trailed off. Behind the blind, the shape seemed smaller suddenly, contracted, as though whatever had filled it a moment ago had withdrawn. "Forgive me. I cannot always remember. The words come, and then they go, and I am... I am not certain which ones I spoke and which ones I only thought."

Something cold moved through my chest. Not fear—I did not name it fear—but something adjacent to it, a wrongness that my body registered before my mind could articulate. The woman behind that screen had spoken in two voices. One weak and dying. One clear as a temple bell.

The incense swelled around me, cloying, sweet to the point of rot. Beneath it—beneath the plum, beneath the smoke—something else lurked. Something the perfumed air was working very hard to hide.

I should leave. The thought surfaced clearly, a single bright warning in the fog of my fascination. This house was cursed, or haunted, or something else entirely that priests should examine and nobles should avoid. The woman behind the screen was ill—ill in body, perhaps ill in mind—and I had no business sitting here, breathing this poison air, listening to a voice that was not one voice but two.

I did not leave.

"My lady," I said instead, "may I return tomorrow?"

The pause that followed lasted long enough that I thought she had not heard me, or had heard and would not answer. Then the blind moved—not lifted, but pressed outward from within, as though a hand had rested against it from the other side.

"Yes." The voice was weak again, grateful, almost desperate. "Yes. Please. I have been alone for so very long. The days... the days do not pass here as they should. I think it has been spring for months. Or perhaps years. I cannot—"

She broke off. A dry sound that might have been a cough or might have been a sob.

"I will return," I said. I rose to my knees, bowed again toward the screen, and began to make my way back through the corridor. The plum scent followed me like a living thing, clinging to my robes, filling my lungs.

At the entrance, the Attendant waited. She did not look at me as she slid the screen aside to let me pass. Her face was composed, blank, the strange pale crescent in her eye invisible in the dim light.

"Thank you," I said, uncertain why I was thanking her.

"The Lady values your company." Her voice carried no inflection. "Your visits bring her... stability."

The word was wrong. I knew it was wrong even as she spoke it. Stability implied something out of balance, something that needed correction. But the Attendant's face revealed nothing, and I was already stepping out onto the white gravel path, already drawing the night air into lungs that ached for something clean.

Behind me, the perfect house stood silent. The lanterns burned without flickering. The plum trees held their impossible blossoms against the autumn sky.

I would return tomorrow. I could not have said why.
