# Period Rules: ch20-noir-detective

## Historical Setting
- **Period:** 1930s-1940s (Noir era)
- **Location:** Los Angeles, California
- **Year:** 1938 (Specifically January–March, during the "Great Flood" and the height of the Shaw administration's corruption)

## Genre

**Detective Noir - Core Definition:**

Detective noir is not just "dark detective fiction." It's a moral framework expressed through crime investigation, where:
- The mystery is solvable, but the world is not fixable
- The detective uncovers truth, but truth rarely restores order
- Justice, if it appears at all, is partial, compromised, or personally costly

**Key Distinction from Golden Age Detective Fiction:**
- Golden Age (Christie, Sayers): puzzle → solution → moral reset
- Noir: investigation → revelation → moral residue. The stain remains.

**Canonical Influences:**
- Dashiell Hammett (The Maltese Falcon, Red Harvest)
- Raymond Chandler (The Big Sleep, Farewell My Lovely)
- James M. Cain (Double Indemnity, The Postman Always Rings Twice)

**Noir Conventions Required:**
- Hard-boiled detective fiction aesthetic
- Moral ambiguity, cynicism, fatalism
- Urban decay, corruption, institutional failure
- Shadow and light (prose equivalent of cinematographic noir)
- Femme fatale archetype present (Lilith's manifestation)
- Cases that corrupt or destroy those involved
- Voice: first-person or close third, sardonic/weary tone
- The city as antagonist/labyrinth
- Violence is inevitable, not climactic
- Violence doesn't resolve—it escalates or confirms futility

**The Deep Structure (Three-Act Scaffolding):**

**Act I — The Hook with Rot Underneath**
- A simple job: missing person, blackmail, suspicious death
- The detective accepts for pragmatic reasons
- Early signal: this case is already poisoned
- Function: establish tone + personal stakes, not plot complexity

**Act II — Descent Through Layers of Complicity**
- Each clue implicates someone more powerful
- Every answer creates a worse question
- Allies become liabilities
- The detective's code is tested repeatedly
- Important: The mystery advances, but moral clarity erodes

**Act III — The Truth (and Its Cost)**
- The detective solves the case
- The truth does not save anyone
- Justice is partial, delayed, or symbolic
- The detective survives—but diminished
- Often ends with: walking away, burning a bridge, choosing silence over exposure

**Thematic Engine:**
Central question: *What does integrity look like in a world that punishes it?*

Recurring themes:
- Alienation
- Complicity
- The price of knowing
- The illusion of control
- Ethics without redemption

**Critical Rule:** If the reader feels cleaner at the end, you did it wrong.

## Historical Constraints

### Technology

**Telephones:**
- Heavy black bakelite rotary phones
- Public payphones require a nickel (5¢)
- "Dictographs" (primitive listening devices) used by police "Spy Squad" to wiretap private residences
- No answering machines, no caller ID
- Long-distance calls expensive, operator-assisted
- Party lines common (shared phone lines between households)

**Automobiles:**
- 1938 Chevrolet Master Deluxe or Ford V8 (common detective cars)
- **Buick Roadmaster** (1936+ model, luxury car, $900-1,000 new)
- Features: running boards (for action beats), manual transmission, center-hinged hoods
- Vulnerable to car bombs wired to the starter
- No seat belts, no power steering, no turn signals
- Starting car requires: key, choke adjustment, possibly crank on cold mornings

**Firearms:**
- **Detective:** Colt Detective Special (.38 snub-nose), easily concealed in coat pocket
- **Police:** Smith & Wesson Model 10 or Colt Official Police (.38 Special)
- **Hitmen:** Thompson submachine guns ("Chicago Typewriters") or sawed-off shotguns
- **Ammunition:** No hollow points (not invented yet), full metal jacket rounds
- **Concealed carry:** Permits issued by Sheriff/Police Chief, often political favors

**Forensics:**
- **Ballistics:** The "Comparison Microscope" is established tech—bullets can be matched to specific guns via rifling striations
- **GSR (Gunshot Residue):** The "Paraffin Test" (hot wax on hands) detects gunpowder residue, but is notoriously unreliable—false positives from tobacco ash or fertilizer
- **Fingerprinting:** Available but not yet computerized—manual comparison only, requires clean prints
- **Photography:** Crime scene photos exist but are slow to develop (no instant cameras)
- **Blood typing:** Can determine ABO blood type, but not DNA
- **Autopsies:** Available, but often cursory unless high-profile case
- **No DNA, no security cameras, no digital records, no trace evidence databases**

**Communication/Record-Keeping:**
- Typewriters (Remington No. 5 portable standard for PIs)
- Carbon paper for copies (messy, limited copies)
- Physical files only—filing cabinets, manila folders
- Newspaper morgues (archives) for research
- City directories (reverse phone lookup by address)
- Hall of Records for property deeds, marriage licenses, voter rolls
- All research requires physical legwork—no internet, no databases

**Investigation Tools:**
- Flashlight (heavy metal, requires large D-cell batteries)
- Magnifying glass
- Lock picks (illegal, but common)
- Notebooks and pencils
- Camera (large, requires flashbulbs)
- Bribery (often more effective than tools—$5-10 buys most information)

### Social Structure

**Class Dynamics:**
- **Bunker Hill:** Decaying Victorian mansions now boarding houses for the poor/transient
- **The Flatlands/Hollywood:** Wealth, new construction, Art Deco glamour
- **Angels Flight:** Funicular railway connecting the slum on the hill to the business district below—physical symbol of class divide (5¢ fare)
- Stark visibility of Depression-era poverty alongside new oil/film money
- "Hoovervilles" still exist on city outskirts
- Dust Bowl refugees ("Okies") arriving, desperate for work
- Class mobility nearly impossible—you die in the class you're born to

**Racial Segregation:**
- Rigid geographic segregation enforced by law, covenant, and violence
- **Central Avenue:** "Harlem of the West"—vibrant hub of African American life and jazz (Club Alabam, Dunbar Hotel)
- White characters typically only visit for nightlife or illicit dealings
- Segregated housing (redlining, restrictive covenants), employment, public facilities
- Police brutality particularly severe in non-white neighborhoods
- Interracial relationships illegal (anti-miscegenation laws)
- Japanese-Americans face increasing hostility (pre-internment)
- Mexican-Americans concentrated in East LA, face deportation threats
- **Critical:** Avatar's race affects every aspect of experience and available choices
- **Jack Malone as white male:** Has privilege, access, but also scrutiny from corrupt system

**Gender Roles:**
- Women in workforce due to Depression, but limited to secretarial, service, factory work
- Professional women (lawyers, doctors) extremely rare and face institutional barriers
- Women cannot get credit without male co-signer
- Married women often can't own property in own name
- Domestic violence not recognized as crime ("family matter")
- Abortion illegal (but available through underground networks)
- Single women face moral scrutiny ("loose" vs. "respectable")
- Femme fatale archetype: woman using sexuality as power in male-dominated world

**Police/Private Detective Relationship:**
- **Hostile by default**
- LAPD is the antagonist, not ally
- Chief James "Two-Gun" Davis operates a "Gun Squad" that shoots criminals on sight
- Private Detectives viewed as threats to the corrupt "Combination" (city government + mob)
- P.I.s regularly harassed, beaten, arrested on trumped-up charges, or bombed by police
- **Harry Raymond bombing (Jan 14, 1938):** Former detective investigating city corruption survives car bomb planted by LAPD Spy Squad—sets tone of paranoia
- Anyone starting their car is risking death
- Ex-cops like Malone: viewed as traitors, snitches, refuse

**Organized Crime Landscape:**
Three-way power struggle:
1. **Tony Cornero:** Floating gambling ships (S.S. Rex in Santa Monica Bay, water taxis from pier)
2. **Jack Dragna:** Local Mafia boss (controls vice in Little Italy)
3. **Eastern Syndicate:** Bugsy Siegel expanding westward (future Las Vegas)

**Mayor Frank Shaw's "Combination":**
- City government takes cut of all vice operations (gambling, prostitution, liquor)
- Police protect mob interests, shut down competitors
- Reform movements violently suppressed
- Recall movement building throughout 1938 (will succeed—first recall of major U.S. mayor)
- Political tension: reformers vs. Combination = violent struggle
- Anyone investigating corruption risks Raymond-style car bombing

**Speech Patterns:**

**Detective voice (Jack Malone):**
- Laconic, cynical, economical
- Uses understatements and similes ("He looked about as happy as a termite in a yo-yo")
- Deflects personal questions with wisecracks
- Short sentences, tough-guy syntax
- Observational detail rendered in metaphor
- Internal monologue: philosophical, weary, darkly humorous
- Example: "The rain came down like God's own punishment. I didn't mind. I was already wet."
- Occasionally drops grammar for effect ("He don't care," "We was friends once")
- Profanity mild by modern standards (Hell, damn, bastard—stronger words rare)

**Femme Fatale (Lilith):**
- Double entendres, sexual tension in subtext
- Husky low register (described, not written phonetically)
- Speaks in imperatives or questions that trap the listener
- Uses pauses, hesitations for effect
- Mixture of vulnerability and control
- Formal diction mixed with street smart
- Example: "You could help me, Mr. Malone. If you wanted to. If you're the kind of man I think you are."

**Police/Authority:**
- Aggressive, bureaucratic brutality
- Dismissal of P.I. as nuisance or threat
- Threats thinly veiled as advice
- Casual violence in language
- Example: "Nice office you got here, Malone. Be a shame if something happened to it. Or to you."

**Criminal/Underworld:**
- Coded language, euphemisms
- Elliptical references (assume listener knows context)
- Mixture of formal and crude
- Example: "The boss wants to have a conversation. The kind you don't walk away from."

**Class Differences:**
- Working class: dropped g's ("goin'," "nothin'"), contractions, slang
- Upper class: formal diction, complete sentences, affected British tones
- Malone: code-switches depending on who he's talking to

### Historical Events (January–March 1938)

**The Harry Raymond Bombing (January 14, 1938):**
- Former LAPD detective Harry Raymond investigating city corruption
- Car bomb planted by LAPD "Spy Squad" (Captain Earle Kynette)
- Raymond survives but severely injured
- Bomb wired to car starter—common mob/police tactic
- Establishes tone of paranoia: starting your car = risking death
- Galvanizes reform movement against Shaw administration
- Proves the Combination will kill to protect itself
- **Narrative use:** Jack Malone is aware of this, knows his corruption case is Raymond-level dangerous

**The Great Flood of 1938 (February 27 – March 3):**
- Two massive storms hit Los Angeles back-to-back
- LA River (largely unpaved) jumps its banks
- Over 100 people killed
- Bridges collapse (Lankershim, others)
- Rail lines washed out, telegraph lines down
- Downtown streets become rivers of brown mud
- Neighborhoods isolated, cut off
- Bodies surface, evidence washes away
- City literally dissolving
- **Atmosphere:** Rain-soaked, mud-caked, apocalyptic noir
- **Narrative use:** Perfect backdrop for climax—chaos, confusion, bodies lost in flood (or bomb victims)

**The Recall Movement:**
- Growing public outcry against Mayor Frank Shaw
- Fueled by Raymond bombing and corruption revelations
- Reformers organizing petition drives
- Combination fighting back with violence, intimidation
- Will succeed later in 1938 (first recall of major U.S. mayor)
- Creates political tension/instability throughout period
- Reform newspapers vs. controlled press
- **Narrative use:** Jack's corruption case ties into this movement—reformers may hire him

**Background Context:**
- Great Depression still crushing working people (10 years in)
- Unemployment still high despite New Deal programs
- Dust Bowl refugees arriving daily (Okies)
- Labor organizing met with police violence (Red Squad)
- Hollywood Golden Age producing glamorous facade (while city rots)
- Europe moving toward war (background dread, newspaper headlines)
- Hitler's annexations, Spanish Civil War, Japanese expansion
- Americans anxious about being drawn into war

**Daily Life Details (What People Were Doing):**
- Listening to radio: news, The Shadow, soap operas
- Going to movies: double features, newsreels (25¢)
- Reading pulp magazines: Black Mask, Weird Tales, detective fiction
- Smoking constantly (cigarettes everywhere, ashtrays in every room)
- Riding streetcars (Pacific Electric Red Cars) to work
- Burning trash in backyard incinerators (creating smog)
- Worrying about money, rent, next meal
- Looking for work, any work

## Avatar Profile: Jack Malone

**Full Name:** Jack Malone (John Patrick Malone)

**Born:** 1900 (age 38 in 1938)
- Born in Los Angeles (Irish-American working class)
- Father: Dockworker, union man, died in industrial accident (1912)
- Mother: Seamstress, died of influenza (1919)

**Background:**
- WWI service: U.S. Army, 1917-1918 (age 17-18, lied about age)
- Fought in France, saw combat (Belleau Wood, Meuse-Argonne)
- Returned 1919, no family left (mother dead), no prospects
- Joined LAPD 1920 (age 20)—needed structure, paycheck, purpose
- Beat cop → Detective Division by 1928
- Reputation: good cop, good detective, played by the rules
- **1936: Quit the force** after witnessing corruption he couldn't stomach
  - Specific incident: ordered to cover up evidence in murder case (Combination-connected suspect)
  - Refused, threatened with demotion/termination
  - Quit before they could fire him
  - Walked away from pension, seniority, everything
- **1936-present:** Private investigator, barely scraping by
- Office on Bunker Hill, cheap SRO room, taking cases that pay

**Occupation:** Private Detective
- License: California Private Detective License (bonded, legal)
- Office: Second floor, Bunker Hill, above Angels Flight
- Specialties: Infidelity, missing persons, skip-tracing, background checks
- Reputation: Ex-cop, honest (liability in corrupt city), competent but unlucky
