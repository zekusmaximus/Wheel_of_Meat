The first fly arrived with the sun.

I heard it before I saw it—a low drone circling the water jug, investigating. The heat was already thick, pressing against the mud-brick walls of my cell like something with weight. I counted the fly's orbits. Three, four. Then it landed on the lip of the jug, and I watched its legs test the clay's rough edge, the way its body trembled with the effort of stillness.

My knees had gone numb against the stone floor sometime in the third hour of prayer. The numbness was familiar now, almost a companion—the body announcing its presence through absence, the way silence announces itself through the things it swallows. I let the fly drink. Water was scarce, and I would drink from the same lip later, sharing without intention what the desert made precious.

The morning office had ended, but my lips still shaped the words. *Lord Jesus Christ, have mercy on me.* The prayer had worn a groove in my mind over fifteen years, smooth as the path from my cell to the well. I no longer said it so much as became the vessel through which it said itself. This was the theory. In practice, my thoughts still scattered—the ache in my hip, the heat building toward its midday fury, the satisfying roughness of the palm fibers waiting in the corner.

I rose slowly, one joint at a time. The body protested, as it always did, and I noted the protest without judgment. This too was practice. The flesh complained; the spirit observed the complaint. The gap between sensation and reaction was where God lived, or so the elders taught. I had found something there, though I could not have named it. A kind of space. A breath between the body's demand and the mind's response.

The palm fronds lay in their pile, already cut and soaking in a shallow basin. I settled onto the woven mat beside them, arranged my legs, and began to work. The motion was automatic now—split, twist, weave, pull tight. My hands knew the pattern better than my mind did. I had made thousands of these baskets and would make thousands more, and there was a pleasure in the making I did not examine: the pleasure of a thing done flawlessly, without witness, for fifteen unbroken years. Basket after basket, month after month, carried to the village and traded for bread and oil. The economy of the desert was simple: solitude cost labor. I paid willingly. I paid more than most. Few of the brothers had lasted as long, or as cleanly, and I was not above knowing it.

The palm fronds themselves were a gift—old Didymus's hands could no longer manage the cutting, but his eye for quality fiber remained keen. Every basket I made contained this silent partnership: one man's judgment, another man's labor. The desert broke those who refused to need each other; this much I had learned in my first hard years.

A lizard appeared in the doorway, paused at the threshold of shadow and light. Its throat pulsed once, twice, then it was gone—a flicker of gray-brown against the sand, too quick to follow. My hands continued their work. The cell was four paces by three, walls whitewashed long ago and now yellowed by sun and dust. A reed mat for sleeping. The water jug. A wooden cross, hand-carved and smooth from years of touching. These were my possessions, if possession was even the right word for things that owned me as much as I owned them.

The heat pressed closer. Sweat beaded at my temples, traced the lines of my face and disappeared into my beard. The white hair was a surprise still, though I had watched it come strand by strand over the years. I remembered brown. I remembered a young man who could argue philosophy in three languages, who had worn fine linen and eaten well. That man was dead—not metaphorically but actually, the way a caterpillar dies into the moth. What remained was something else, something I had not quite named, though I confess I believed it finer than what it had replaced.

My fingers found a bad section of fiber, brittle where it should bend. I set it aside. The pile of discards grew slowly, a small monument to imperfection. Nothing was wasted; even the rejected fibers would become kindling or fill gaps in the walls. The desert taught economy because it offered nothing else.

A gust of wind moved through the cell, stirring the dust, carrying with it the faint smell of the Nile somewhere impossibly distant. I paused. Closed my eyes. Let the air move across my skin and registered each sensation—the cooling of sweat, the slight roughness of sand particles, the way my robe shifted against my shoulders. This too was prayer, the elders said. Attention itself was holy. To notice the wind was to notice the one who sent it.

The basket took shape beneath my hands. It would hold perhaps a measure of grain, perhaps dried fish. Someone in the village would buy it, and the coins would become bread, and the bread would become enough to continue. The circle was unbroken, had been unbroken for fifteen years. I was the old man in the cave now, the one the younger monks whispered about. *Macarius of Scetis. Macarius the patient. Macarius who had given everything up.* I let the names pass through me the way I let the prayer pass through me, and if they left a warmth behind, I called it gratitude and did not look closer.

I worked. The fly returned, or perhaps a different fly. The sun climbed. The heat became something to breathe rather than merely endure—thick, visible in the shimmer above the sand beyond my door. I drank once, carefully, feeling the water move down my throat and settle into my stomach. The body was grateful in its wordless way.

By midday the basket was nearly finished. I would rest through the worst of the heat, then return to prayer, then perhaps visit the well if my water ran low. The pattern was fixed, predictable, and in its predictability I found a kind of peace. Not the peace of feeling—I had learned to distrust feelings—but the peace of structure. The day held me as the basket held whatever was placed inside it. Shape contained. Boundaries defined.

The silence of the desert was not silent. Wind across sand. The creak of palm fronds. Somewhere, very distant, the call of a bird I could not name. These sounds filled the silence without breaking it, the way stars fill the night without dispelling the darkness. I had learned to listen for what was not said. The desert spoke in negation, in the space between sounds, in the absence that made presence possible.

My hands finished the last row of weaving and tied off the ends. I held the basket up, examined it in the slant of light through the doorway. Serviceable. Not beautiful, but then beauty had never been the point. The point was the doing, the discipline, the faithful repetition that wore grooves in the soul like prayer wore grooves in the mind. I set the basket aside with the others—four now, ready for the next trip to the village.

The stone floor was cool despite the heat. I lay back on the mat, felt my spine settle, vertebra by vertebra, into the thin cushion of reeds. Above me, the palm-thatch roof stirred faintly in whatever breeze reached this far. A single beam of light fell across my feet, illuminating the dust that hung suspended in the air.

I breathed. The breath entered and left without instruction. The body knew its business, had been knowing it since long before I decided to pay attention. I watched it work—the rise and fall of my chest, the pause between inhalation and exhalation, the small clicks of my throat adjusting. Somewhere in that machinery, a soul was supposed to live. I had been trying to find it for fifteen years. Some days I thought I had. Most days I was less certain.

The heat pressed down like a hand. I let it.

The desert watched, and waited, and said nothing at all.
