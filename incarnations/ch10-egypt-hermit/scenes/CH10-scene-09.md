Time passed. I did not count it.

The delegation had returned to Alexandria, taking their tablets and scribes and the woman whose eyes had held something I still could not name. The young monks continued to visit, but I sent them away without words. The pilgrims found an empty threshold. The merchants left their gifts untouched. Even Paphnutius, persistent as hope, eventually stopped coming.

The routine I had maintained for fifteen years did not resume. It had been, I understood now, a structure built on false foundations. Without the pride to support it, the discipline collapsed into meaninglessness. I ate when my body demanded food. I drank when my throat contracted with thirst. I slept when exhaustion made wakefulness impossible. Beyond these animal necessities, I did nothing.

The baskets sat unfinished in their corner. The prayer beads hung untouched. The cross on the wall watched me with wooden patience, waiting for a devotion that no longer rose.

I was, very slowly, dying.

The realization came without drama—not as a revelation but as a simple recognition, the way one notices the sky has clouded over or the wind has changed direction. My body had been aging throughout the fifteen years, accumulating the small damages that desert living exacted, and without the will that had driven it, the system was winding down. I could feel the slowing in my breath, the weakness in my limbs, the way each morning brought less energy than the one before.

I did not fight it.

What was there to fight for? The structure I had built was rubble. The purpose I had served was exposed as vanity. The holiness I had cultivated was revealed as its opposite. If this body wanted to return to dust, I would not stand in its way. The soul—if there was such a thing, if I had ever truly understood what the word meant—could make its own arrangements.

But the insight remained.

This was the strange thing, the unexpected mercy, if mercy it was. The recognition that had shattered my self-image had not departed with the self-image. I still saw what I had seen: the structure of my pride, the pattern of my life, the way fifteen years of renunciation had been fifteen years of acquisition by other means.

And in seeing this, in holding the truth of it without flinching, something had changed that I could not quite identify. The thing I had glimpsed through the ruins—the fragile, nameless beginning—was still there. It had not vanished with the collapse. It had, perhaps, only become possible because of the collapse.

I did not know if this was theosis. I suspected not. Union with God, as the fathers described it, came with clarity and peace and the luminous certainty of the divine presence. What I felt was different—darker, more ambiguous, shot through with grief and exhaustion and the raw ache of having been profoundly wrong about myself.

But it was true.

Whatever this was, it was true in a way that my fifteen years of discipline had not been true. It was true the way pain is true, the way hunger is true, the way the body's insistence on its own needs is true. It did not ask me to believe in it. It simply was.

The days shortened, or perhaps my awareness of them shortened. I lost track of how long I had been lying on my mat, how many times the sun had risen and set, how many weeks had passed since the gathering where my pride had been exposed. Time had become strange—elastic, folding back on itself, running ahead and then returning like a dog uncertain of its master's direction.

The illness found me in this condition.

It came first as a trembling in my muscles—not the trembling of emotional upheaval, but the trembling of fever's early stages. Then a heat in my skin that had nothing to do with the desert sun. Then a thickness in my lungs that made each breath an effort.

I lay on my mat and watched the progression with distant interest. The body was failing. It had carried me for decades, through Alexandria's lecture halls and the desert's privations, and now it was reaching the end of its capacity. The flame that had animated it was guttering.

*This is death.*

The thought came with a clarity that caught me off-guard. I had expected fear, perhaps, or resignation, or the grasping bargaining I had witnessed in others approaching this threshold. Instead, there was only observation—the same quality of attention I had brought to the fly drinking from my water jug, to the basket taking shape beneath my fingers, to the subtle movements of my own mind during prayer.

I was dying. I was watching myself die. The two facts coexisted without contradiction.

But as the hours passed and the fever deepened, something else began to rise through the observation—something I had not anticipated, something the insight of the gathering had not prepared me for.

The body remembered.

It remembered things I had forbidden it to remember, things that fifteen years of discipline had suppressed and starved and tried to kill. They came back now, in the loosening of consciousness that fever brought, in the thinning of boundaries between memory and dream.

Color.

The memory struck with physical force. Color—not the endless beige of sand and stone and sun-bleached cloth, but *color*. The blues of Alexandria's harbor, catching light, shifting with the movement of water. The reds of market stalls, fabrics piled in towers of crimson and scarlet. The greens of the Nile's edge, the living green of growth, of abundance, of life that did not merely survive but flourished.

I had forgotten what color looked like.

I had starved myself of it so thoroughly that the memory now felt like revelation.

Texture came next. The smoothness of marble under bare feet. The roughness of papyrus beneath my fingers. The give of a cushion beneath tired legs—I could almost feel it now, the way my body had sunk into softness, the way comfort had cradled me without asking anything in return.

Softness. I had not touched anything soft in fifteen years. My mat was reeds. My robe was coarse wool. My walls were mud and stone. I had lived surrounded by hardness, by edges, by surfaces that resisted the body rather than welcomed it.

*Why?*

The question surfaced through the fever's haze. Why had I done this to myself? Why had I forbidden my body its simple pleasures, denied it everything that made flesh worthwhile?

Because I thought holiness required mortification. Because I thought the body was the enemy of the soul. Because I believed that deprivation was the price of transcendence.

And these beliefs were wrong.

They were—I saw it now, saw it with the same terrible clarity that had revealed my pride—they were just another form of violence. I had not transcended my body; I had punished it. I had not released my grip on pleasure; I had built a grip on pain. The structure was the same. Only the furniture had changed.

The pendulum.

The word surfaced from somewhere—a pattern the fathers described, a rhythm in the spiritual life. When one extreme is held too long, the release creates momentum toward the opposite extreme. The ascetic who starves himself, upon dying, craves abundance. The hermit who refuses beauty, upon dying, hungers for color.

I felt the hunger now.

It was not abstract, not philosophical, not the subject for a teaching. It was visceral, urgent, the body's final and furious protest against everything it had been denied. Fifteen years of deprivation coalesced into a single, overwhelming ache.

*I did not live.*

*I did not touch. I did not taste. I did not see.*

*I made myself a ghost before my time, and now I am dying, and I have missed—*

*I have missed everything.*

The fever leached color from my vision, but the memory of color burned brighter. My hands, lying limp on the mat, remembered the textures they had never touched. My skin, weathered and dry, remembered the warmth of bodies it had never held.

*I wanted holiness. I received pride.*

*I wanted transcendence. I received exhaustion.*

*What I wanted—what I truly wanted—*

My hands moved. I did not instruct them to move; they moved on their own, the body's final reaching. They stretched toward the doorway where the light was fading, toward the world that continued without me, toward something that was not there and could not be there because I had spent fifteen years ensuring it would never be there.

Softness.

Color.

Life.

My fingers grasped air. My body shuddered with the effort.

And beneath the shudder, beneath the fever, beneath the grief and the insight and the terrible recognition of waste—beneath all of it, something else stirred.

Something more honest than peace, more raw than theosis, more truthful than the resolution I had hoped my death might bring.

A fierce, animal longing for everything I had refused.

The beauty I had called distraction.

The pleasure I had called temptation.

The body I had treated as enemy rather than home.

I wanted them now with a clarity that cut through every teaching, every discipline, every word I had ever offered to the monks who sought my wisdom.

*Let me be surrounded by beauty.*

*Let me touch what is soft.*

*Let me live in color.*

*Let me not waste the gift again.*
