Night came, though in the cell night and day were distinguished only by subtle shifts in the grey light that seeped through the high window—darker grey, lighter grey, the prison's own twilight that never truly lifted or settled but simply existed as a permanent condition. I lay on the straw pallet, my body too exhausted to sleep, my mind too active to rest. The hatred pulsed beneath my breastbone with each heartbeat—steady, persistent, almost comforting in its constancy.

I examined it the way I would examine a wound. What was it made of? Where had it taken root? The physician's diagnostic approach applied to the condition of my own interior state, as though cataloguing symptoms might reveal treatment or at least understanding.

Tomás's face appeared first. The hollow eyes. The shaking hands. The desperate need for absolution I had refused to give. The hatred for Tomás was specific, personal—rage at a man who had chosen his son over his friend, who had carried twenty years of shared secrets to the Holy Office like tithe, who watched from his window while I walked to imprisonment bought with testimony that could not be retracted or forgiven.

But the hatred did not stop with Tomás.

The inquisitors with their gentle voices and patient questions. The scribe whose quill transformed lives into evidence. The guards who performed their duty with mechanical precision. The woman in grey who watched from corners with eyes that seemed to see past the surface of things to some truth I could not name but felt like weight pressing against my chest. The Holy Office entire—the machinery that consumed the innocent and guilty alike, that made no distinction between devotion and performance because performance was always suspect and devotion could never be proven.

And myself. The physician who could not stop healing. The useful man who had believed usefulness would protect him. The performer who had lived forty-three years in careful safety purchased with constant vigilance, only to discover that vigilance purchased nothing and safety was illusion and the bargain had never existed except in my own desperate need to believe competence might armor flesh against the world's cruelty.

The hatred encompassed all of it. Burned bright and hot in my chest, warming the cold cell, giving me something to hold when everything else had been stripped away. My only possession. My only certainty in a place where certainty dissolved under questioning and truth became whatever the inquisitors needed it to be.

I could carry this hatred to my death. The thought arrived clearly, without particular emotion. I could hold it close, nurse it, let it burn in my chest until the fire consumed me literally and the hatred became one with the flames. The inquisitors would burn my body, but they could not burn the rage I carried—that would remain mine, inviolate, the one thing they could not extract through questioning or persuasion or machinery designed to make flesh confess what mind refused.

Except. Except.

The hatred burned, yes. Warmed me, yes. But what did it illuminate?

My physician's mind turned the question over with the same care I had used examining the hatred's roots. What did this rage show me? What did it reveal?

Tomás had betrayed me. This was fact. But Tomás had also been given an impossible choice—his son's life purchased with his friend's freedom. What father would choose differently? What man could watch his child burn and not pay whatever price was demanded? The choice was understandable. Human. The kind of terrible calculus the Holy Office specialized in creating—moral dilemmas with no clean answers, situations where any choice carried guilt.

Understanding did not make it forgivable. I tested this recognition, probed it for weakness. No. I understood Tomás's choice completely and forgave nothing. But understanding and forgiveness were not the same thing, and hatred and understanding could exist together without canceling each other.

The inquisitors. They believed their work holy. Genuinely believed it—I had seen the conviction in their questions, the certainty that extracting confession served God's purpose and saved souls even when it destroyed bodies. They were not demons. They were men who had been given authority and convinced that authority served righteousness, who never questioned whether their certainty might be mistaken because questioning would undermine the foundation upon which the entire machinery rested.

Hating them was easy. Understanding them was harder. But understanding them—seeing how ordinary men became instruments of systematic cruelty through the conviction that they were serving good—did not require forgiving them. Did not require releasing the rage their questions provoked.

Unless.

The thought arrived quietly, almost hesitantly, like a patient presenting symptoms they were not certain merited the physician's attention.

Unless the hatred hurt me more than it hurt those I hated.

I sat up on the pallet, the movement sudden enough to make my head swim. The cell spun briefly before settling into familiar stone walls and grey light and the bucket whose smell I had stopped noticing through constant exposure.

Tomás would live with what he had done. Would wake every morning to my face in memory. Would carry the weight of betrayal for whatever years the Holy Office granted him before finding some other reason to consume him. My hatred added nothing to that burden. Changed nothing. Tomás's guilt was his own, and whether I hated him or not made no difference to the calculus of his suffering.

The inquisitors would continue their work. Would question and examine and extract confessions from other prisoners with the same patient dedication they brought to my case. My hatred of them changed nothing. They would not know I hated them. Would not care if they did. The machinery ground forward independent of its subjects' feelings toward it.

And myself. The self-hatred for hands that healed when healing served evil, for skills that made me useful, for the bargain I had never made but had spent my life believing in—that hatred poisoned nothing except myself. Made no one else suffer. Changed no external circumstance.

The hatred existed only in my own chest. Burned only in my own interior country. Hurt only me.

This recognition settled in my mind with the clarity of diagnosis after long examination. The hatred was real. The reasons for it valid. But carrying it forward accomplished nothing except ensuring that the Holy Office's work continued inside me after my body burned, that Tomás's betrayal kept betraying me through my own refusal to set down what Tomás had handed me, that I remained prisoner to rage even if—when—they released my body to secular authorities for final disposition.

I could choose to carry it. Or I could choose to set it down.

Not forgiveness. The distinction mattered. Forgiveness implied reconciliation, implied making right what had been done wrong, implied some restoration of relationship or trust. I could not forgive Tomás. Could not forgive the inquisitors. Could not forgive the Holy Office or the woman in grey or any of it.

But I could release them. Could open the fist clenched around the burning coal of hatred and let it fall. Not because they deserved release. Not because the wrongs they had done were acceptable or understandable or anything except what they were—cruelty, betrayal, the systematic destruction of human beings in service of an ideology that claimed righteousness.

But because carrying the coal burned only my own hand. And burning accomplished nothing except ensuring my suffering continued independent of theirs, that I remained bound to them through hatred long after death severed all other connections.

I touched my fingers together in sequence. Index. Middle. Ring. Smallest. The physician's ritual. The gesture that steadied.

My hands trembled, but less than before.

The hatred remained. I could feel it in my chest—solid, hot, real as any wound I had ever treated. But I was no longer identical with it. Some space had opened between the Diego who felt rage and the Diego who observed the rage, between the emotion and the one experiencing the emotion.

I could let it go.

Not all at once. Not completely. Hatred accumulated over forty-three years of suppression and performance and exhausted vigilance could not be released in a single moment of recognition. But the fist could begin to open. The grip could loosen. The coal could be acknowledged as burning without being held.

Tomás had chosen his son. The choice was understandable and unforgivable both. I released him to live with it.

The inquisitors believed their cruelty holy. The belief was genuine and monstrous both. I released them to their certainty.

The Holy Office ground forward, consuming the innocent. The machinery existed independent of my rage toward it. I released it to continue without my hatred fueling its fires from within my own chest.

And myself. The physician who healed because he was a physician, whose skill was deeper than choice, whose usefulness had never protected him but was, nevertheless, what he was. I released the hatred of my own hands. They were simply hands. They had done what they were trained to do. The evil was in the use others made of them, not in the hands themselves.

The woman in grey. Her watching eyes. Her presence in corners. The wrongness I felt when she was near. Something about her remained unresolved—some recognition my body held that my mind could not grasp. But hating her accomplished nothing. I released her to whatever purpose brought her to interrogation chambers and Toledo streets. She was not my burden to carry.

Something loosened in my chest. Not the hatred disappearing—it remained, would likely always remain, the scar tissue of these weeks that no healing could fully mend. But it was no longer me. I had stepped back from it. Become the one who observed the hatred rather than the one consumed by it.

The coal fell from my hand. I watched it fall. Felt the burning ease.

Outside the cell, the prison continued its machinery. Prisoners suffered. Inquisitors questioned. Scribes recorded. The Holy Office pursued its holy work of purification through destruction, and my release of hatred changed none of it.

But it changed me.

I lay back on the pallet, my body settling into straw that crunched and released familiar smells. My hands rested at my sides—steady now, or steady enough. The physician's hands that had never protected me but which I no longer hated for failing to accomplish what hands alone could never achieve.

Through the high window, the stars were visible—countless and cold, the same stars that had looked down on my grandfather's grandfather, on generations who had hidden their prayers and performed their faith and died anyway because the world did not care about performance or devotion or usefulness, only about blood and who had permission to shed it.

I had released them. Tomás and the inquisitors and the machinery and my own hands and the woman whose eyes watched from corners. Released them all to whatever waited—for them, for me, for the days between now and the fire I knew was coming.

The hatred remained. But I was free of it.

Free to die without rage poisoning the final moments. Free to face whatever came next without carrying the burden forward into new flesh, new life, new chances to learn what this life had finally taught me:

That hatred was a choice. That persecution could destroy the body but could not poison the soul unless the victim drank the poison himself. That release was possible even when forgiveness was not. That setting down the burning coal did not mean accepting what had been done, only refusing to let it continue doing itself inside me long after the external cruelty ended.

I closed my eyes. Exhaustion claimed me—not the grinding wakefulness of recent days but something deeper, more restful, the first true sleep since the knock had split my old life from this new truth.

I slept without dreams. Without hatred's heat keeping me wakeful.

Free, in the only way freedom remained possible in this place where every other freedom had been stripped away.

Free to die peacefully. To burn without rage. To carry forward nothing except the question that was already forming in the space where hatred had lived:

If I could release this—the deepest, most justified rage I had ever known—what else might be released? What else was I carrying that did not need to be carried? What other certainties might be questioned, examined, set down when they proved to be burdens rather than truths?

The question would wait. For now, I slept. Released.
