## Parallel Structure: THE COMPULSION

**Avatar's mistake:** Conflates selflessness with self-destruction; martyrdom compulsion; cannot distinguish service from suicide. "One life is worth the effort" becomes the lie that kills him.

**Lilith's parallel mistake:** Compulsive tracking; cannot stop even when recognizing kinship; sacrifice of everything to this pursuit. She tells herself it's strategy. She tells herself it's necessary.

**The devastating irony:**
- Philon saves one woman and loses the hundred
- Lilith tracks one thread and loses... what? Her life? She's not risking that. Her connection to anything else? That's already gone.
- Both of them are *choosing* isolation while telling themselves they're serving something larger
- Both of them mistake their personal compulsion for virtue

**How to show it:**
> Philon could not stop reaching for the dying. Lilith could not stop reaching for the thread. Both gestures wearing the face of duty.

**Where the parallel breaks visible:**
- When he carries the slave woman toward his clinic
- Her tracking corner, the thread she cannot release
- Both of them alone with something they cannot save

## Intervention Details

**Method 1:** Accelerates xenophobic rumor (plague from Persian ships) to create panic and strain resources

**Method 2:** Sends dream to Alexios showing Philon's corpse; whispers that survival is service—Alexios abandons Philon

**How each registers sensorially:**

For Method 1: The rumor spreads like infection. Lilith feels the panic rising as a tightening—not in her chest (too abstract) but as the crowd's fear bleeding through, as bodies pressing tighter in the Long Walls, as the volume of coughing multiplying.

For Method 2: Dream-sending is intimate. She presses close to Alexios's sleeping mind. The dream is visceral—Philon's body on a pyre, the particular smell of burning mentor, the heat on Alexios's dream-face. When he wakes, he remembers the smell.

**Objective:** Isolate Philon, accelerate martyrdom, corrupt lesson through despair

**Backfire:** Isolation crystallized rather than corrupted; concentrated karma; question "Was the one for them—or for me?" became pure seed of insight

**How she experiences the backfire:**
When Philon dies with that question burning in his chest, Lilith should feel something—not pain, but *recognition*. The same question she's not asking herself: Is the tracking for them, or for me?

She files it. Returns to analysis. Does not finish the thought.

## The Grossness: Don't Flinch

The plague is not metaphor. The plague is:
- Pustules that swell and burst and weep
- Fever that cracks lips and leaves patients begging
- Bodies that swell in doorways, gathering flies
- The smell—sweet and thick and inescapable
- Skin that mottles and discolors in patterns
- Lungs filling with fluid that rattles with each breath
- Eyes glassy with fever, rimmed with inflammation
- Hands that claw weakly at stone
- The creak of joints as someone tries and fails to move

Lilith experiences this through Philon's hands. The physician who cannot stop touching. Every texture of dying registers in her own body—not as pain, but as data, as sensation, as the particular weight of mortality she has transcended but cannot ignore.
