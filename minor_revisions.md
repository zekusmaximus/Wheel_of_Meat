Problems (ranked by severity)
1. Lilith's "canonical face" is too conspicuous — Malone sees too much
Period rules explicitly require: "He barely notices her — just a face that feels strangely familiar but he can't place." And: "No attraction, no relationship, no femme fatale dynamic."

The draft violates this:

Bar (line 187–189): Malone describes "widow's peak, the single darker strand that fell across her forehead" and has "a sense of recognition."
Corner (line 395–399): He sees the widow's peak and the pale-green crescent in her left eye across a street — which the profile says is "visible in good light" up close, not from fifty feet in gray rain.
Alley (line 831): Again at 50 ft in rain, he sees "a pale crescent of green" in her iris.
This is too much recognition, and it functionally pulls her toward femme-fatale orbit. He also implicitly chases the thread (line 401: "rooted to the spot, unable to shake the feeling that she'd been waiting for me"). The profile is explicit: she is a face he cannot place, not one he tracks.

Recommended fix: Strip the green-crescent detail from appearances #2 and #3. Keep it implicit for the reader (canonical face cues) but keep Malone's perception generic ("dark-haired woman I'd seen somewhere," not an inventory of distinguishing marks). The green eye should be noticed by the reader through repetition of silhouette, not by Malone forensically.

2. The blackmail subplot has drifted into a separate case with femme-fatale overtones
The profile's subplot #2 is: "Client being blackmailed — discovers client guilty of worse, blackmailer turns up dead (made to look like Malone did it), police harassment escalates."

The draft delivers the broad beats, but invents Gloria Dane (line 471–514) — a sequined-dress nightclub singer with "full lips" whose scene is rendered with sexual framing. She functions aesthetically as a femme-fatale ghost-echo. Given period-rules.md's explicit carve-out that Lilith is not the femme fatale and no femme fatale subplot should exist, Gloria blurs that line. She doesn't need to be a cabaret singer in a sequined dress — she can be a bookkeeper's mistress seen somewhere drab.

Also: the blackmail case introduces "Howard Brennan," which collides with the reformer "David Brennan" introduced on line 32. Two Brennans is a copyediting error that will confuse readers.

3. Past-life bleed-through is too explicit
The profile says he should not consciously access prior lives until the death sequence. But:

Line 4: The crowd-rally flashback ("dust, blood, bodies too close together") — bleed-through from an earlier life, fine as a whisper.
Line 51: The office smells of "dust and incense and too many bodies packed into a space meant for prayer" — Taiping bleed-through, also acceptable.
Line 427–429: "In France—" I blinked. In France? I'd never been to France." This is a flat contradiction. The profile establishes Malone fought in France in WWI (Belleau Wood, Meuse-Argonne). He absolutely has been to France. This line needs to be cut or recast as a non-WWI France (i.e., a prior-life France — but then the "I've never been to France" beat makes no sense given his service). Most likely a draft error where the author forgot the WWI backstory.
Line 417: "I've seen those eyes before" — too close to overt recognition. Should be pushed back to the death scene.
4. Combination sabotage explicitness vs. Lilith's invisible hand
The profile's framework: the Combination is the real antagonist; Lilith only accelerates their existing actions from distance. The draft mostly respects this, but at a few points Malone directly theorizes a non-Combination agent:

Line 391: "This wasn't the Combination's usual style — they preferred blunt force… This was surgical. This was personal."
Line 601: "Someone who had more power than the Combination, more reach than the police, more patience than any enemy I'd ever faced."
This edges Malone toward suspecting a supernatural/distant operator, which contradicts the profile's insistence that the Combination plants the bomb and Malone dies believing the Combination did it. The surgical-precision read gives the reader a nudge toward Lilith that isn't warranted and risks making Malone look obtuse for not following through. Soften these — let the pattern of failure feel like the Combination's ordinary malice plus Malone's own bad luck.

Also: Chief Davis is never on-page, and Captain Kynette is named only once (line 47). For a corruption-expose chapter, these antagonist names should echo more than once.

6. The Ch21 handoff needs sharpening
Ch21 assumes Lilith's Ch20 observation produces horror at completed perfections, compressing her timeline from "a week" to "NOW." The draft's final image of her (line 933) is right — she is "receding… her eyes fading from strange to ordinary to invisible" — but there is no interior beat confirming her horror, no "all ten complete" recognition rendered from her side.

Given this is first-person Malone POV, her horror can't be rendered directly, which is fine. But the narrator's coda (lines 951–971, after Malone dies) shifts to omniscient and has room to plant the Ch21 seed. Currently it says only "the wheel turned" and "everything had changed." A single phrase locating Lilith's dawning realization — "A woman stood on the sidewalk and understood, too late, what she had just witnessed" — would make the Ch21 opening ("I have to find him NOW") land as causal rather than coincidental.

7. Ch19 handoff (minor)
Ch19 ends with Lilith needing "the data from Jack Malone's tracking" before approaching Silas. The draft never signals that she is tracking him — her three appearances read as observational presence only. This is probably fine (she's watching from 2032-33 as future-Lilith; "tracking" is retrospective), but a note in the Ch20 opening from the omniscient coda — or removing "tracking" language from Ch19 — would tighten the through-line.

8. Period/detail slips
Line 311: "a three-year-old Roadmaster I'd bought cheap from a widow" — the Buick Roadmaster launched as that nameplate in 1936. A "three-year-old" Roadmaster in March 1938 would be a 1935 car, which predates the Roadmaster name. Either make it "a couple of years old" or move the purchase date to 1936/37 explicitly. (Period-rules.md: "Buick Roadmaster (1936+ model).")
Line 312: The Murphy-bed room is established as on Bunker Hill in an SRO with a shared bathroom down the hall. By line 311, Malone is parking in the alley behind my boarding house and climbing stairs directly to his room — the geography seems to skip the shared-bath/SRO landlord infrastructure. Not wrong, just underspecified for the period texture the rules demand.
Line 15: Malone drinks whiskey alone in his room from "the bottle I kept in my nightstand." The profile's SRO says no in-room liquor bottle is specified, but the office has one in the desk drawer — standard noir. This is fine; just note the draft doubles the bottle across two settings.
The flood section is thin: period-rules.md gives rich Great Flood detail (Lankershim bridge, Pacific Electric washouts, 100+ dead, city dissolving). The draft compresses this to three paragraphs (lines 615–645). Given the profile calls this a "perfect backdrop for climax," the flood should be more sensory — mud on shoe-tops, bodies in trees, the smell, the NG troops. Right now it reads like telling-not-showing.
No Clifton's Cafeteria visit. The profile names Clifton's specifically ("Eats at Clifton's"). A single scene there — the fantasy-forest decor, pay-what-you-can — would nail the 1938 LA flavor.
No Shadow on the radio, no pulp magazines, no Angels Flight ride except the morning descent in scene 1. Habits from the profile should recur.
"Someone has to give a damn" is repeated nine times. Profile uses it as a spine-phrase, so frequency is intentional, but by line 763 it has started to feel like a stamp rather than a refrain. Consider varying two or three instances.
